To enjoy football, all you need is a ball, a few friends, and a spacious area to play. If you got the skill and aspire to go pro, you can enroll in an affordable academy, compete in competitions, impress scouts, before you know it, you could be on your way to signing your first contract with a decent European club. Even if the opportunity to play for the big clubs doesn't come, you've still experienced the sport in its entirety.

However, the same cannot be said for the average person who wants to experience racing. Why? Because it's expensive. Karting is one of the more affordable ways to compete in motorsport, but you can't just go to the track and start driving around. You'll need a kart, tires, equipment, and even a team to manage everything on and off the track. It can cost anywhere from $5,000 to $10,000 in club and national leagues and potentially more in national events.

But fret not, we've got sim racing. What's sim racing, you may ask? It's simulated racing that uses software to accurately model real-life data, physics, cars, and tracks. All you need is a console or computer and a set of wheels and pedals, and you're ready to experience everything the world of racing has to offer from the comfort of your home. No other sport complements its real-life counterpart like sim racing does.

It's no secret that Formula 1 teams employ in-house sim racing software to train their drivers and even field esports teams that compete in sim racing events. Take, for instance, Max Verstappen, a two-time Formula 1 champion who races for Team Redline participates in top-tier sim racing esports tournaments, including the Le Mans Virtual Series, Porsche Supercup Esports Series, and IMSA Esports.

Jann Mardenborough, James Baldwin, Jimmy Broadbent, and Cem Bölükbaşı, to name a few, are real-life drivers who began their careers in sim racing. You might wonder how that's possible. Sim racing teaches drivers the nuances of car handling, race strategy, mastering the racing line, and much more. Unlike real-world racing, where there's limited time on the track for practice and the risk of physical injury to the driver and damage to the vehicle, sim racing offers unlimited practice time, and no one gets hurt from crashing their virtual Toyota Tom's Castrol Supra race car.

So, if your bank account isn't geared up for car racing, give sim racing a spin. It's a fraction of the cost, and you get to enjoy racing your favorite virtual cars with your friends on the simulation of your choice. And who knows, if the stars align, you might find yourself with a shot at real-life racing, armed with some solid experience earned from the comfort of your home.
